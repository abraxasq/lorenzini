Most decisions look better when you stop at the first-order effect.

The first-order effect is immediate and legible: if I do X, then Y happens. Y is usually the thing I want. The chain seems short.

Second-order effects require another move: *and then what?* If Y happens, what else changes? Who responds? What equilibrium shifts?

## Why We Stop at First Order

First-order thinking is faster. It produces a decision now, which feels like progress. Second-order thinking is slower and introduces uncertainty — there are more branches, and the branches have different probabilities you may not know precisely.

There is also a social cost to being the person who asks *and then what?* in meetings. It reads as resistance or pessimism, even when it is just rigor.

## A Simple Heuristic

Before finalizing any significant decision, ask: if this works exactly as intended, what is the most important thing that changes as a result? And what does that change cause in turn?

You do not need to trace every branch. You need to trace the one that is biggest and most likely, and make sure you are prepared for it.

Often this reframes the decision significantly without requiring you to abandon it.
