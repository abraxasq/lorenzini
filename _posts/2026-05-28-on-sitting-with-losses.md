The loss is already there. It is not a loss until you sell, in the accounting sense, but in every other sense it is already real — the capital is impaired, the thesis is under pressure, and the question is what to do next.

The decision to hold, reduce, or exit has nothing to do with the price you paid. That price is gone. The only relevant question is: given what I know now, is this the best use of this capital?

## Why This Is Hard

Prospect theory explains part of it: losses feel larger than equivalent gains, which creates a bias toward avoiding the realization of losses even when the rational action is to realize them.

But there is also something else — a social and psychological commitment to the original thesis. To sell is to admit the thesis was wrong, and the ego resists that admission, sometimes fiercely.

## The Discipline

I try to review losing positions the same way I would review a new idea. Forget the entry price. Forget the original thesis. Ask: if someone described this security to me today, at its current price, with what I currently know, would I buy it?

If the answer is yes: hold, and potentially add.
If the answer is no: sell.
If the answer is uncertain: the position is probably too large.

This is easier to write than to do. The practice is in the asking.
