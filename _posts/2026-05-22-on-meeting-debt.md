Technical debt is well-understood: shortcuts taken now that create compounding friction later. Engineers track it, argue about it, and sometimes allocate sprints to reduce it.

Meeting debt is the same phenomenon applied to coordination overhead, and almost no organization accounts for it.

## How It Accumulates

A recurring meeting starts with a real purpose. The purpose is eventually addressed — or the project ends, or the team changes — but the meeting persists. Nobody cancels it because cancellation requires an initiating act, and inertia is cheaper in the short run.

Six months later there are eleven recurring meetings on the calendar, six of which address problems that no longer exist in their original form.

The people in those meetings are not stupid. They are navigating the same coordination pressure that created the meetings originally, just with less signal about whether the vessel still holds water.

## The Audit

Every quarter, I try to cancel at least one meeting that cannot clearly answer the question: *what would fail if this stopped happening?*

Often the answer is: nothing, immediately. Sometimes: a relationship would fray that is worth maintaining through a different channel. Occasionally: something real would break.

The third category keeps the meeting. The first two are candidates for reduction or elimination.
