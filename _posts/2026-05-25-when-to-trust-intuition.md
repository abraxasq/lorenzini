Intuition is pattern recognition operating below the level of verbal articulation. You have seen something similar enough times that the signal fires before you have assembled the argument.

This makes it useful in some conditions and dangerous in others.

## The Conditions for Reliable Intuition

Research on expert judgment suggests intuition becomes reliable when:

1. The environment provides rapid, accurate feedback — you learn quickly whether you were right
2. You have had enough repetitions to build a real pattern library
3. The patterns you encountered in training are similar enough to the current situation

Firefighters develop reliable intuition about structural collapse. Chess grandmasters develop reliable intuition about position. These are domains with clear feedback, many repetitions, and consistent underlying structure.

## Where It Fails

Intuition fails in novel situations — ones that look familiar but operate by different rules. It fails when the feedback loop is long or noisy. It fails when the pattern library was built in a biased sample.

The market is a good example. Many participants have strong intuitions about how prices move. Those intuitions were often built in a particular market regime that has since ended. The pattern library is real but the environment has changed.

The discipline is to ask: in what domain was my intuition trained, and is this situation actually in that domain?
