A dashboard is a confession of what you have decided to care about. Every metric on it is a vote; everything left off is a vote too.

I have rebuilt mine three times this year, and each version told me less about the world and more about myself.

## What Earns a Place

A number earns a place on the morning dashboard only if it can change a decision. If I would do exactly the same thing whether the figure is high or low, it is not a metric — it is decoration.

The test I apply to each line:

1. **Is it actionable?** Does a move in this number change what I do today?
2. **Is it leading or lagging?** Lagging metrics confirm; leading metrics warn. I want at least a few of the latter.
3. **Can I trust the source?** A precise number from a bad pipeline is worse than a rough number I understand.

## The Current Layout

The version I run now fits on one screen, by design:

- **Cash and runway** — the only number that is allowed to wake me up
- **Open positions and their sizing** — weights, not prices
- **One leading indicator per domain** — the early-warning row
- **A single "what changed since yesterday" note** — written by hand, not generated

The handwritten note is the part that matters most. The automated rows tell me the state of things. The note tells me what I think about the state of things — and that is the input the next decision actually runs on.

> A dashboard does not make decisions. It makes the cost of ignoring reality slightly higher than the cost of facing it.

Most mornings that small nudge is enough.
