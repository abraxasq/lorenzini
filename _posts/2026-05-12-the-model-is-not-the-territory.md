Every mental model is a simplification. The map omits features of the terrain that did not seem important when the map was drawn. This is not a flaw — it is the definition of a model. A map that included every feature of the terrain would be the size of the terrain.

The problem arises when the model's omissions are invisible to the person using it.

## Compression and Its Costs

Good models compress reality efficiently. They let you navigate without reprocessing everything from first principles every time.

Bad models compress reality by hiding the parts that are inconvenient to include — usually the parts that falsify the model.

The indicator of which kind you have is your response to disconfirming evidence. If you find yourself explaining why the exception does not count, the model may be compressing by exclusion rather than by simplification.

## The Update Habit

I try to track cases where my expectations were wrong, specifically. Not outcomes I did not want — those are just regret. Outcomes that *surprised* me, that I did not see coming given the model I was running.

Surprise is a reading. It means the map and the territory diverged. The next step is not to discard the map but to ask which feature it failed to represent, and whether that feature can be added.

This is slower than defending the model. It produces better models.
